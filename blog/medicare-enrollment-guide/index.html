Medicare Enrollment Guide: What You Need to Know

By Jack Estes DeBrabander · February 10, 2026 · Estes Health Solutions

Medicare is the federal health insurance program that provides coverage for Americans aged sixty-five and older, as well as certain younger individuals with disabilities or specific medical conditions. While Medicare provides essential coverage for millions of people, understanding its structure, enrollment rules, and supplemental options can be genuinely challenging. At Estes Health Solutions, Jack Estes DeBrabander has helped hundreds of clients navigate Medicare with clarity and confidence. In this comprehensive guide, Jack Estes DeBrabander walks you through everything you need to know about Medicare enrollment, from the basics of each part to the critical decisions that affect your coverage and costs.

The Four Parts of Medicare Explained

Medicare is divided into four distinct parts, each covering different aspects of healthcare. Jack DeBrabander explains each part to every Estes Health Solutions client during their initial Medicare consultation, because understanding the full picture is essential to making the right choices.

Part A: Hospital Insurance

Medicare Part A covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice care, and some home health services. Most people qualify for premium-free Part A if they or their spouse paid Medicare taxes for at least ten years (forty quarters). Jack Estes DeBrabander notes that while Part A has no monthly premium for most enrollees, it does include a deductible for hospital stays and coinsurance for extended inpatient care. Estes Health Solutions helps clients understand these cost-sharing requirements so they can plan accordingly.

Part B: Medical Insurance

Medicare Part B covers outpatient care, doctor visits, preventive services, medical equipment, and certain home health services. Unlike Part A, Part B requires a monthly premium, which is adjusted based on your income. Jack DeBrabander emphasizes to his Estes Health Solutions clients that Part B also involves an annual deductible and typically requires the beneficiary to pay twenty percent of the Medicare-approved amount for most services. This twenty percent coinsurance is unlimited, which is why many people seek supplemental coverage.

Part C: Medicare Advantage

Medicare Advantage plans, also known as Part C, are offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare. These plans combine Part A and Part B coverage into a single plan and often include additional benefits such as prescription drug coverage, dental, vision, and hearing services. Jack Estes DeBrabander at Estes Health Solutions explains that Medicare Advantage plans typically have network restrictions, requiring you to use doctors and hospitals within the plan's network. However, they often feature lower out-of-pocket costs and an annual out-of-pocket maximum, which Original Medicare (Parts A and B alone) does not provide.

Jack DeBrabander recommends that clients carefully compare Medicare Advantage plans with Original Medicare plus a Medigap policy to determine which structure provides the best overall value for their healthcare needs and budget. This is one of the most important decisions in the Medicare enrollment process, and Estes Health Solutions provides the detailed analysis needed to make it with confidence.

Part D: Prescription Drug Coverage

Medicare Part D covers outpatient prescription medications through plans offered by private insurance companies. Each Part D plan has its own formulary, which is the list of covered drugs organized into cost tiers. Jack Estes DeBrabander stresses the importance of reviewing a plan's formulary before enrolling, as the medications you take must be covered at an affordable tier for the plan to provide real value. Estes Health Solutions runs detailed drug coverage analyses for every Medicare client, comparing their specific medications against multiple Part D plans to identify the most cost-effective option.

Understanding Medicare Enrollment Periods

Medicare enrollment is governed by specific time windows, and missing the right one can result in gaps in coverage or permanent premium penalties. Jack Estes DeBrabander carefully tracks these enrollment periods on behalf of every Estes Health Solutions client to ensure no one misses a critical deadline.

Initial Enrollment Period (IEP)

Your Initial Enrollment Period is a seven-month window that begins three months before the month you turn sixty-five, includes your birthday month, and extends three months after. Jack DeBrabander advises his Estes Health Solutions clients to enroll during the first three months of this window to ensure coverage starts as early as possible. Delaying enrollment beyond this period without qualifying coverage can result in late enrollment penalties that increase your Part B and Part D premiums for as long as you have Medicare.

General Enrollment Period (GEP)

If you missed your Initial Enrollment Period, the General Enrollment Period runs from January 1 through March 31 each year, with coverage beginning on July 1. Jack Estes DeBrabander notes that enrolling during the GEP typically means a gap in coverage and may include the aforementioned late enrollment penalties. Estes Health Solutions works to ensure clients never find themselves in this position through proactive planning.

Special Enrollment Period (SEP)

Special Enrollment Periods are available in specific circumstances, such as when you lose employer-sponsored coverage, move to a new area, or qualify for certain assistance programs. Jack DeBrabander at Estes Health Solutions helps clients identify whether they qualify for a SEP, which provides flexibility outside the standard enrollment windows without penalty.

Annual Open Enrollment Period (AEP)

The Annual Open Enrollment Period runs from October 15 through December 7 each year. During this window, Medicare beneficiaries can switch from Original Medicare to a Medicare Advantage plan, change Medicare Advantage plans, switch back to Original Medicare, or join, switch, or drop a Part D prescription drug plan. Jack Estes DeBrabander uses this period to review each Estes Health Solutions client's coverage and recommend adjustments based on changes in health needs, medications, or plan offerings.

Supplemental Coverage: Medigap Policies

Original Medicare (Parts A and B) leaves significant cost-sharing gaps, including deductibles, coinsurance, and copayments. Medicare Supplement Insurance, commonly known as Medigap, is sold by private insurance companies to help cover these out-of-pocket costs. Jack Estes DeBrabander considers Medigap planning one of the most important consultations he provides at Estes Health Solutions.

Medigap policies are standardized and labeled with letters (Plan A, Plan B, Plan C, Plan D, Plan F, Plan G, Plan K, Plan L, Plan M, and Plan N). Each letter corresponds to a specific set of benefits defined by Medicare, so Plan G from one insurance company covers exactly the same benefits as Plan G from another. The only differences are the premium and the company's customer service. Jack DeBrabander helps Estes Health Solutions clients compare premiums across carriers for their desired Medigap plan to find the best value.

One critical detail that Jack Estes DeBrabander emphasizes is the Medigap Open Enrollment Period, which begins the month you are sixty-five or older and enrolled in Part B. During this six-month window, insurance companies cannot deny you coverage or charge higher premiums due to pre-existing conditions. Jack DeBrabander strongly recommends that every eligible Estes Health Solutions client purchase their Medigap policy during this guaranteed-issue period.

Medicare Advantage vs. Original Medicare with Medigap: How to Decide

This is the single most consequential decision in the Medicare enrollment process, and it is where the expertise of Jack DeBrabander at Estes Health Solutions proves most valuable. Both paths have distinct advantages and tradeoffs.

  • Medicare Advantage typically offers lower monthly premiums, additional benefits (dental, vision, hearing, fitness), and an annual out-of-pocket maximum. However, it comes with network restrictions and may require prior authorization for certain services. Jack Estes DeBrabander recommends Advantage plans for clients who are comfortable using network providers and want a single, streamlined plan.
  • Original Medicare with Medigap provides the broadest provider access, as almost all doctors and hospitals in the United States accept Original Medicare. Combined with a Medigap policy, out-of-pocket costs become highly predictable. However, you will need a separate Part D plan for prescription drugs, and the combined premiums for Part B, Medigap, and Part D are typically higher than a Medicare Advantage premium. Jack DeBrabander at Estes Health Solutions recommends this path for clients who travel frequently, have complex medical needs, or strongly value unrestricted provider choice.
"There is no universally right answer when it comes to Medicare Advantage versus Original Medicare with a supplement. The best choice depends entirely on your individual health needs, your preferred providers, and your financial situation.
